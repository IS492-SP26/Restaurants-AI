# Paper: Augmented Entrepreneurship: The Role of AI Agents in Automating Core Small Business Functions
Ganapathi, J. K. (2025). Augmented Entrepreneurship: The Role of AI Agents in Automating Core Small Business Functions. Sarcouncil Journal of Engineering and Computer Sciences, 4(8), 654–663.
DOI: https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.16887026
PDF: https://sarcouncil.com/download-article/SJECS-395-2025-654-663.pdf
# Summary:
This paper addresses the problem that small business owners face increasing operational complexity due to fragmented software systems across accounting, marketing, customer management, and compliance. The author argues that while AI is already used in many business tools, it is mostly limited to isolated task automation rather than coordinated decision-making. The paper proposes the concept of augmented entrepreneurship, where multiple specialized AI agents collaborate across business functions under human oversight. Using a systems level analysis of existing small business software platforms, the paper evaluates their readiness for multi agent orchestration and identifies technical and organizational gaps. The findings suggest that orchestrated multi agent AI can significantly reduce coordination burden, improve cross functional decision support, and enable scalable growth without increasing human labor. However, the paper emphasizes that successful adoption depends on careful system design that preserves transparency, human control, and entrepreneurial skill development.
# Insights:
1.	I learned that one of the biggest challenges for small business owners is not a lack of tools, but the coordination burden created by too many disconnected tools, which AI agents could help manage holistically.
2.	The idea of “orchestration-first” design stood out to me, where AI agents are designed to communicate and share context across functions instead of acting as isolated assistants.
3.	I found it insightful that multi-agent AI systems shift the entrepreneur’s role from hands on execution toward strategic oversight, which could fundamentally change what skills future founders need.
# limitations / risks: 
1.	A major risk discussed in the paper is error propagation, where a mistake made by one AI agent could cascade across multiple business functions and amplify its negative impact.
2.	Another limitation is the risk of over-reliance on AI, which could decline skills and reduced situational awareness for entrepreneurs if too much operational knowledge is abstracted away.
# Idea this inspires for my project:
This paper directly inspires the potential design of our project’s multi agent system, where each AI agent represents a different expert role (market analysis, financial planning, operations, and risk assessment) for planning a business. Instead of generating isolated outputs, these agents could share a common business context and coordinate their recommendations, while still allowing the user to approve or modify high impact decisions.

# Paper: Artificial Intelligence and Strategic Decision-Making: Evidence from Entrepreneurs and Investors
Csaszar, F. A., Ketkar, H., & Kim, H. (2024). Artificial Intelligence and Strategic Decision-Making: Evidence from Entrepreneurs and Investors. Strategy Science, 9(4), 322–345.
DOI: https://doi.org/10.1287/stsc.2024.0190
PDF: https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/stsc.2024.0190
# Summary:
This paper studies whether modern AI systems, specifically large language models (LLMs), can meaningfully support or replicate strategic decision-making traditionally performed by entrepreneurs and investors. The authors investigate this by comparing AI generated and AI evaluated business strategies with those created and assessed by humans in real entrepreneurial contexts. Using data from a startup accelerator and a startup competition, they experimentally test whether LLM generated business plans attract similar or greater investor interest compared to human-written plans. They also compare LLM evaluations of business plans with evaluations from experienced venture capitalists and angel investors. The findings show that AI generated strategies perform at least as well as those produced by entrepreneurs and that AI evaluations are strongly correlated with investor judgments. Overall, the paper concludes that AI can significantly augment strategic decision making by improving the speed, scale, and consistency of strategic analysis, while still requiring human oversight.
# Insights:
1.	I learned that AI generated business plans can receive equal or even higher investor interest than human written plans, especially for lower quality initial ideas, suggesting AI can meaningfully raise the baseline quality of entrepreneurship.
2.	The paper clarified how strategic decision making can be broken down into cognitive processes like search, representation, and aggregation and how AI specifically enhances each of these processes.
3.	I found it interesting that AI may be most valuable not as a replacement for top entrepreneurs or investors, but as a scalable strategist that helps explore many more options than humans realistically can.
# limitations / risks:
1.	A key limitation is that LLMs rely heavily on past data, which can lead to conventional or overly generic strategies and may reduce strategic novelty if humans over trust AI outputs.
2.	There is also a risk that widespread adoption of AI driven strategy tools could intensify competition and reduce competitive advantage, since many firms may converge on similar AI recommended strategies.
# Idea this inspires for my project:
This paper directly supports our idea of using AI agents to help plan a new business by showing that AI can already generate and evaluate business strategies at a human-comparable level. For our project, this suggests designing agents that act as a strategist, critic, and evaluator mirroring the paper’s use of AI for strategy generation, devil’s advocacy, and investment evaluation while keeping the human user in control of final decisions.
